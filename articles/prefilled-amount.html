QR Amount Fraud

The Pre-Filled Amount Trap

📖 4 min read 🛡 SatarkScan Awareness
⚠ How the scam works
You Expect To Pay
₹150
Auto Rickshaw Fare
→ BUT
The QR Demands
₹1500
Hidden Parameter: am=1500

Scammers encode an extra zero into the QR code, hoping you won't read the screen carefully.

The real scenario

You take an auto rickshaw. The agreed fare is ₹150. At the end of the ride, you pull out your phone and the driver shows you a QR code on his device, saying "Just scan and pay."

You scan it. Your UPI app opens instantly with the amount already filled in. Because you are in a hurry, you glance at the screen, see the "1" and the "5", tap "Pay", and enter your PIN. A second later, you realise ₹1,500 just left your account. The extra zero cost you ten times the fare.

How does this work?

UPI QR codes can be generated dynamically to include specific parameters, including a pre-filled amount (using the `am=` parameter in the QR data). This is a legitimate feature meant to make billing faster at supermarkets or toll booths.

However, scammers abuse this by generating a custom QR code on their phone with a deliberately inflated amount. This scam relies entirely on human psychology. Our brains take shortcuts; when we expect to pay ₹150, we often just visually confirm the presence of those digits and ignore the trailing zero, especially in a rush or under pressure from the vendor.

👀

Always read the final amount aloud before entering your PIN.

Never trust a pre-filled amount from an unverified source. Verify every single digit.

🚩 Red flags to watch for

🚩
The amount is already filled in when you scan the QR for a service where the price varies (like an auto rickshaw or local market stall).
🚩
The pre-filled amount is suspiciously round (e.g., ₹1,000, ₹5,000) for a small, casual purchase.
🚩
The vendor insists you scan quickly, acts impatient, or tries to distract you by talking while you are on the payment screen.
🚩
The vendor shows you the QR code on their own mobile phone screen rather than pointing to a printed, laminated code on their desk.

✅ How to stay safe

✅
The 3-Second Rule: Stop for exactly three seconds and read the digits on your payment screen one by one before typing your PIN.
✅
Edit the amount: If a QR code has a pre-filled amount, you can usually tap on it and edit it back to the correct amount before paying. If the app doesn't let you, cancel the transaction.
✅
Use SatarkScan: Scan the QR code using the SatarkScan app first. Our engine specifically parses the `am=` parameter and triggers a HIGH RISK alert if it detects unusually large pre-filled amounts from unverified merchants.
